IMMIGRATION SECURITY SCREENING DELAYS RISE: CSIS REPORT

Overview of CSIS Security Screening Backlog

OTTAWA (August 6, 2025) – The Canadian Security Intelligence Service (CSIS) has reported a significant increase in immigration security screening requests, leading to growing delays that are impacting applicants across Canada.

In its 2024 annual report, CSIS revealed it received over 538,000 screening requests from immigration and border officials, up from 493,200 in 2023 and roughly 300,000 annually over the prior five years. Despite the high volume, CSIS states it will "take the time required" to thoroughly complete each screening.

1. Personal Stories of Delay

Ali Abuhannoud, a Jordanian refugee applying for Canadian citizenship, has been waiting for his security screening result since May 2024, after applying in July 2023. This marks his third screening since arriving in Canada in 2018. Abuhannoud expressed significant stress and anxiety over the prolonged process, highlighting the emotional toll these delays cause.

2. Factors Driving Increased Screenings

The surge is partly attributed to increased asylum seekers, all of whom require security screening. CSIS, Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada, and the Canada Border Services Agency are jointly reviewing the screening process, especially after security screenings missed potential threats, such as the father and son charged with terrorism linked to ISIS in 2024.

3. Legal and Community Concerns

Immigration lawyers across British Columbia and Ontario report rising client frustrations with unexplained delays. Adrienne Smith, a Toronto-based lawyer, notes a lack of government transparency regarding actions taken to justify these delays. Will Tao, a Burnaby immigration lawyer, points to geopolitical concerns disproportionately affecting applicants from Middle Eastern, Ukrainian, Russian, Chinese, Iranian, and Indian backgrounds.

4. CSIS Response and Prioritization

CSIS spokesperson Magali Hébert emphasized that security screening is a critical national security function and that the agency is diligently processing the high volume of requests. The agency also prioritized applications from Gaza and Lebanon due to ongoing regional conflicts.

5. Outlook on Backlog Resolution

Concerns remain that government intentions to reduce immigration numbers may limit motivation to clear the screening backlog. Applicants like Abuhannoud feel unfairly punished for circumstances beyond their control, highlighting the human impact behind the statistics.
